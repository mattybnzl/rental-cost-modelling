The hidden maths of rent

A house has costs -mortgage, insurance, rates, maintenance, management. These costs exist whether the owner lives in it or rents it out. When a landlord rents a property, they need the rent to cover these costs plus a return on their investment. When costs go up, rents go up. But here's the catch: some charges are percentages (like the property manager's fee), so they grow automatically when rent rises -the snowball effect.

Some costs are paid by the landlord (built into your rent). Some you pay directly (power, internet). Some the taxpayer covers (negative gearing offsets, social housing). Toggle any cost below to see what happens when it shifts between bearers.

What do the sliders and presets do?
Market Rent
Sets the weekly rent as a % of the regional median. This is what tenants actually pay -determined by demand, not landlord costs.
Interest Rate Change
Adjusts the mortgage rate up or down from the 6.8% baseline. Affects the largest landlord cost (mortgage interest).
Insurance Increase
One-off percentage increase in building insurance premiums above baseline (not per year). Models a scenario like a cyclone or flood event pushing premiums up. Higher in cyclone/earthquake zones.
Council Rates Increase
Percentage increase in council rates above current levels. NZ rates are rising significantly faster than AU due to water infrastructure costs.
Property Value Change
One-off shift in property value up or down (not per year). Affects insurance premiums, land tax, stamp duty amortisation, and depreciation. Models a market boom or correction.
General Inflation
Annual inflation rate applied to operating costs (maintenance, water, compliance, strata, admin). Does not affect mortgage or insurance.
Cost Pass-Through
How much of cost increases the landlord passes to tenants. 100% = full pass-through. Below 100% = landlord absorbs some. Above 100% = landlord adds margin.
Presets set the sliders to representative historical or scenario values. Rent always resets to the regional median when a preset is applied.

What does this mean? -Policy Trade-offs

These are simplified projections. Real-world outcomes depend on enforcement, market conditions, housing supply, and behavioural responses that models cannot fully predict. Every policy involves trade-offs -there are no cost-free solutions.

This tool shows how costs are allocated -not what rent should be. Actual rent is also driven by supply and demand in your local market.

Supply, demand, and migration: Low vacancy rates, population growth, and immigration volumes all create upward pressure on rents independently of costs. This tool focuses on the cost-push side -the floor landlords need to cover.

All figures are approximate and based on a typical 3-bedroom property at regional median prices. Tax benefits are simplified. NZ figures are in NZD.
